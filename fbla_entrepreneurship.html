Module 01
Initial Capital & Credit
💡

There are two fundamental ways to fund a business: debt financing (borrowing money you must repay) and equity financing (selling ownership for cash). Understanding the tradeoffs is essential for any entrepreneur.

Debt Financing

You borrow money and agree to repay it within a set timeframe at a set interest rate. You owe the money regardless of whether your business succeeds.

  • Bank loans (micro to six-figure)
  • Credit cards (30–60 day float)
  • SBA loans (government-backed)
  • Lines of credit
  • Private lending
Interest = Principal × Rate × Time

Equity Financing

You sell partial ownership of your company in exchange for cash. Investors assume the risk — if the company fails, they lose money.

  • Friends & family
  • Angel investors
  • Venture capitalists (typically $1M+)
  • Selling stock / securities
⚠️

Key trade-off: Equity costs more if your company succeeds (shared profits), but less if it fails (no repayment obligation).

Module 02
Financial Statements

Balance Sheet — "The Snapshot"

Shows a company's financial position at a single point in time. Think of it as a photograph of what you own and owe right now.

Assets = Liabilities + Equity
Assets
Fixed Assets: Land, buildings, equipment
Current Assets: Cash, accounts receivable, inventory, notes receivable, marketable securities
Liabilities
Accounts payable, notes payable, interest payable, wages payable, taxes payable
Equity
Stocks, retained earnings — change in equity essentially = income

Income Statement

Shows results of operations over a period of time (month, quarter, year).

Net Income = Revenue − Expenses
  • Revenue = money flowing in
  • Expenses = money flowing out
  • Tells investors whether the business is profitable

Cash Flow Statement

A company can be profitable but still run out of cash. This statement tracks actual cash movement.

  • Operating activities — day-to-day operations
  • Investing activities — buying/selling assets
  • Financing activities — loans, equity
💡 Improve it by: decreasing accounts receivable, increasing revenue, decreasing expenses

Module 04
The Business Plan
📌

A business plan is a written document outlining a company's future and goals. Entrepreneurs use it to communicate their vision to potential investors and guide decision-making.

  1. Executive Summary Written last but appears first. Covers: mission statement, company info (who/when/where), product overview, growth highlights, current investors, funding sought, and future plans.
  2. Company Description High-level review of the business and market. Explains how your product meets customer needs, who your target market is, and what your competitive advantages are.
  3. Market Analysis Demonstrates industry research. Covers: industry description & outlook, target market details, distinguishing characteristics, market size & forecasted growth, market share, pricing strategy, competitive analysis, and regulatory requirements.
  4. Organization & Management Covers organizational structure, ownership details (names, percentages, forms), management profiles (resumes), Board of Directors qualifications, salaries, and the legal structure of the business (LLC, corp, partnership, etc.).
  5. Service or Product Line Describes specific benefits of your product/service, development stage, product lifecycle, patents/copyrights, and any R&D activity.
  6. Marketing & Sales Covers market penetration strategy, growth strategy, channels of distribution, and communication strategy (how you reach customers).
  7. Funding Request (if applicable) States current and future funding needs and how funds will be used.
  8. Financial Projections Historical data (income statements, balance sheets, cash flow) + forecasted data + ratio and trend analysis.
  9. Appendix Credit history, resumes, product photos, letters of reference, market studies, licenses, permits, patents, legal documents, contracts, leases.

Module 05
Types of Business Structures

Sole Proprietorship

The most common business form. One owner, minimal setup. The owner is the business legally.

✓ Pros
  • Very inexpensive and easy to start
  • Only need to register name + local licenses
  • Can freely mix business and personal assets
✗ Cons
  • Personally liable for company debts
  • Rarely survives the owner's death
  • Cannot sell shares in the company

Partnership

Two or more people form a company, formally (with documents) or informally (on a handshake). Also includes Limited Partnerships where some partners have limited liability and limited input — popular with short-term investors.

✓ Pros
  • Relatively inexpensive to start
  • No minimum tax (unlike corps/LLCs)
  • More capital & knowledge from multiple partners
✗ Cons
  • All owners personally liable for debts
  • Must deal with partners' decisions
  • Handshake deals can cause conflict

C Corporation

The main corporate form in the US. Has the legal power of a person — can be sued, commit crimes, file lawsuits, own property.

✓ Pros
  • Owners protected from personal liability
  • Can go public easily
  • Raise capital via securities
  • Transfer ownership via shares
  • Unlimited lifetime
✗ Cons
  • Most expensive to set up
  • Annual meetings + many formalities
  • Annual fees and state filings
  • Double taxation (corporate + dividend)

S Corporation

A special election of corporation. Requirements: one class of stock, fewer than 100 shareholders, all shareholders must be US citizens (certain nonprofits can also hold shares).

✓ Pros
  • Eliminates double taxation
  • Owners protected from personal liability
✗ Cons
  • All officers/owners must take a salary
  • Annual fees and state filings required

Limited Liability Company (LLC)

The newest corporate form (1960s–1980s depending on state). Combines partnership simplicity with corporate tax benefits.

✓ Pros
  • Few ongoing formalities (no annual meetings)
  • Owners protected from personal liability
✗ Cons
  • Hard to go public (must convert to corp first)
  • More expensive than partnerships
  • Annual fees and state filings

Module 06
Business Ethics
⚖️

Ethics is the study of moral choice and values — choosing between right and wrong. A written Code of Ethics defines the level of ethical behavior expected from individuals and employees.

Responsibilities to Customers

  • Treat all customers with respect and honesty
  • Never exaggerate product merits
  • Inform customers of applicable product dangers
  • Handle all disputes fairly, hearing both sides

Responsibilities to Suppliers

  • Be fair, honest, and respectful
  • Give enough time to fill orders
  • Refuse dishonest or illegal schemes
  • Give a fair reason if changing suppliers

Responsibilities to Creditors/Investors

  • Use borrowed money with caution and care
  • Be honest about losses — never cover them up
  • Maintain transparent financial reporting

Why a Written Code of Ethics?

  • Reminds employees of ethical importance
  • Defines what is considered unethical behavior
  • Builds trust with customers and partners
  • Reduces legal and reputational risk

Module 07
Marketing Concepts

Market Penetration

The percentage of the total public that uses a general product category — not limited to one brand.

📱

Example: The % of all people who use smartphones in general is market penetration.

Market Share

A single company's percentage of the total market penetration.

🍎

Example: Apple's percentage of all smartphone users = Apple's market share.

Market Segmentation → Target Market

The act of dividing a broad market into sub-groups based on shared characteristics, then directing marketing resources toward the most promising group.

Segmentation Types
  • Demographic — age, income, education, gender
  • Psychographic — lifestyle, values, personality
  • Geographic — location, region, climate
  • Behavioral — purchase habits, brand loyalty
Marketing Strategies
  • Market Penetration — grow existing market share
  • Growth Strategy — expand the business
  • Distribution Strategy — channels to reach customers
  • Communication Strategy — how to reach customers

Module 09
Economic Systems

🏕️ Traditional Economy

Family/community-based system that relies on history and rituals to make economic decisions. Self-sufficient, barter-based.

Examples: Aboriginal communities, Native American tribes

🛒 Market Economy (Free Market)

Consumer-driven. The economy is shaped by choices of individual buyers and sellers. Prices determined by supply and demand.

Examples: Most developed countries have elements of this (USA, Japan, UK)

🏛️ Command Economy

Government controls production, prices, and distribution. Private ownership is minimal. The state makes all major economic decisions.

Examples: Cuba, North Korea

⚖️ Mixed Economy

A blend of market freedom and government regulation/involvement. Most real-world economies fall here.

Examples: USA, Japan, most of Western Europe

Module 10
Key Terms, Diagrams & IP

Core Economic Concepts

  • Scarcity — Unlimited wants vs. limited resources; the fundamental economic problem
  • Demand — Consumer desire and willingness to pay for a good/service
  • Supply — Total amount of a good/service available to consumers
  • Equilibrium — Where supply and demand balance, stabilizing prices
  • GDP — Total monetary value of all goods/services produced within a country's borders in a year (includes private/public consumption, government spending, investment, exports minus imports)

Intellectual Property

  • Patent
    Valid for 20 years from date of application. Only effective within the US.
  • Trademark
    Company logos and brand marks. Valid as long as renewed periodically.
  • Copyright
    Protects original creative works for the author's lifetime + 70 years after death.

Important Diagrams

PERT Diagram

Program Evaluation and Review Technique. A statistical project management tool used to analyze and represent tasks involved in completing a project. Shows dependencies and connections between tasks — useful for identifying the critical path.

SWOT Analysis

Evaluates a business or project across four dimensions:

Strengths

  • Internal advantages

Weaknesses

  • Internal disadvantages

Opportunities

  • External advantages

Threats

  • External risks
